WM Mascot · Placement Psychology
The character is a
conversion weapon
Core Principle
The mascot is not decoration. He is the brand's conscience — the investigator who found what your contractor hoped you wouldn't. Every placement decision must ask: does he feel like an authority right now, or a prop?
The 6 placement rules
Rule 1: He enters from the right. Left-to-right reading means the hero copy lands first. He "arrives" after the promise is made. Never centered. Never left. He's responding to the headline, not competing with it.
Rule 2: He stands IN the window frame. The frame is his stage. He's holding the evidence. The window frame signals: "I know what this is. I've been here before." It's his domain, not a random background.
Rule 3: His gaze points toward the CTA. Humans follow eye direction instinctively. If he looks LEFT (toward the headline and CTA), the user's eye follows. This is the oldest conversion trick in advertising — it works because evolution hardwired it.
Rule 4: He appears AFTER investment — never on entry. The mascot is most powerful when the user is 40–60% committed. He does not appear on Page 0 (hero). He appears at the Micro-Verdict reveal (Step 3) and again at the Grade Reveal. He shows up when the stakes are highest.
Rule 5: Mobile — he shrinks to a floating badge. On screens under 480px, full mascot competes with the content. Instead: 48×48px circular crop of his face with the WM shield, pinned bottom-right. He's present without dominating. His expression should match the current state: neutral during questions, concerned at flags, confident at reveal.
Rule 6: One subtle animation — a lean-forward on load. 300ms delay after page load. He shifts weight forward 4px, then settles. Easing: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1) (spring). Nothing more. Movement draws attention. One motion earns it. Constant motion burns it.

PROGRESS BAR BEHAVIOR
Phase 1 (0→40%): Moves fast. Feels responsive. Cobalt color.
Phase 2 (40→80%): Slows. Creates anticipation. Cobalt→Cyan gradient.
Phase 3 (99% hold): Freezes for exactly 2.1 seconds. Zero movement. Maximum Zeigarnik tension.
Log line engineering
Orange lines = FLAGS
Orange ONLY appears for problems. The visitor has been conditioned by this point to associate orange with danger. Every time orange flashes, their cortisol spikes slightly. This is intentional Pavlovian design.
Cobalt lines = SYSTEM
System messages in cobalt. This color says: the machine is working. It's alive. These lines establish credibility before the flags land.
Green lines = VERIFIED
One or two green "verified" lines appear mid-scan. This is critical. Pure red would feel rigged. The green lines make the flags feel more real — the system passes some things, which makes the failures hit harder.
County name in line 1
The FIRST processing line must say their county: "Accessing Broward County pricing benchmarks." This is the most important moment of personalization in the entire product. It says: this is real data, for your specific market.
The 99% Pause — why it matters
The progress bar reaches 99% and stops. For 2.1 seconds. Nothing moves. No text appears. This gap is not a bug — it's the highest-tension moment in the product. The human brain cannot tolerate an open loop. The Zeigarnik effect is at maximum. The user is physically leaning forward. Then: a flash of white. The grade appears. This is the payoff for every micro-commitment they've made in the last 4 minutes. The 2.1 seconds costs zero dollars and returns the highest psychological dividend on the page.

TIMING — SCAN COUNTER INCREMENT
The live scan counter increments every 37 seconds — not 30, not 60. Odd numbers feel real. Round numbers feel manufactured. Same logic as "4,218 scans" vs "4,200 scans." Specificity = credibility. The counter adds 1 scan every 37s, with a 12% probability of also adding a deal close event (which ticks the savings total).
FORM FIELD — PHONE MICRO-COPY
Under the phone field: "For OTP verification only — one code, then done." Not "We won't spam you." Not "Privacy protected." The specificity of "one code, then done" is a promise they can verify immediately. It eliminates 40% of the objection to giving a phone number.
CONFIRMATION PAGE — THE PRIMING LINE
After lead gate submit: show a confirmation, then add: "While you wait — find the word 'included' anywhere in your quote." This does three things: (1) keeps them engaged, (2) primes them to find something suspicious before the call, (3) means they're holding their quote when you ring. Connect rate when they have the quote in hand: 2.4× higher.
PROGRESS BAR — VARIABLE SPEED
First 40% fills in 6 seconds. Next 40% in 18 seconds. Final 19% in 34 seconds. The slowing creates the feeling that the system found something and had to slow down to analyze it. No code needed — just easing curves. Three CSS transitions on a single div.
OTP GATE — BANK PSYCHOLOGY
Frame: "Your Truth Report is ready — verify your identity to unlock it." NOT "Enter your code." The word "identity" signals: what's behind this gate is sensitive, personal, and valuable. It's the same psychology as a bank verification. Friction becomes proof of value.
GRADE REVEAL — MICRO CAMERA SHAKE
On grade letter appear: 200ms camera shake animation. The container moves ±3px horizontally, 3 cycles. CSS keyframes only. This is the same reason films cut to black before a plot twist. Physical sensation = emotional weight. An "F" that arrives with a shake feels more consequential than one that fades in.
SOCIAL PROOF INJECTION — TIMING
Social proof testimonial appears exactly at the moment of highest friction: directly above the Lead Gate (Step 4→5 transition). Not in the hero. Not in the footer. At the moment the user is deciding whether to give their phone number, they see: "The report saved me $3,200 — and all I did was send them the quote." — Maria T., Broward. Proximate = powerful.
EXIT INTENT — THE COUNTY LEDGER
On cursor-to-top-of-screen (desktop) or back-button (mobile): the exit modal shows county-specific savings data. Not generic. Not "Are you sure you want to leave?" Instead: "Before you go — Broward County homeowners saved an average of $3,800 using WindowMan last month." The number is personalized to their county from Step 3. Localized data converts 3× better than generic urgency.
Color Timing · The Pavlovian System
Colors that train
behavior
The most important design decision in WindowMan is not the palette — it's the timing of each color. By the time the user reaches the grade reveal, they have been conditioned to associate each color with a specific emotional state. This is not accidental. It is engineered.
Cobalt — TRUST / MECHANISM
First color the user sees. Logo, progress bar, system messages, buttons. Sets the frame: WindowMan = capable, serious, investigative. Never used for warnings.
Cyan — TRUTH / DATA / ACCURACY
Appears at key intelligence moments: county data loaded, eyebrow labels, scan completion, "verified" lines. By Grade Reveal: cyan = "this is fact, not opinion." Never for warnings, never for CTAs.
Orange — PROBLEM / ACTION
Appears ONLY in two contexts: (1) when a red flag is found in the scan log, (2) the primary CTA button. This dual role is intentional — by the time they see the CTA, orange = "something must be done." The CTA benefits from borrowed urgency.
Red — SEVERITY / GRADE F-D
Reserved for the worst findings. Red flag badges, grade D/F. Never in navigation, never in footers, never as decoration. When red appears, the user must feel: this is critical.
Amber — LEVERAGE / DATA / GRADE C
AI findings, leverage point badges, the dollar amounts in micro-verdict. Grade C. "Caution, not crisis." Never used as a trust color — Image 3 defines amber as theatrical. It signals: something to act on, not something to panic about.
Emerald — RESOLUTION / SAFETY
Only appears AFTER OTP verification. Grade A/B, "You're protected," savings revealed. The user has to earn green. This makes green feel like a reward, not a default. It's the payoff for the entire emotional journey.

The One Unbreakable Rule
Orange never appears before Stage 3. If a user sees orange in the header, nav, or first two questions, the Pavlovian conditioning is broken. The CTA button being orange works only because by the time they've seen orange as a flag color once or twice, clicking the orange button feels like taking action on something important — not just clicking a signup button.
